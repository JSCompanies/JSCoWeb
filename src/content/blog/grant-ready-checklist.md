Applying for grants without being grant-ready is one of the most common — and costly — mistakes in the nonprofit world. You spend weeks on a proposal, the narrative is strong, the need is real, and then you get a rejection because your financials aren't audited, or your board is too small, or you don't have a strategic plan on file.

Grant-readiness is about more than a compelling story. It's about organizational infrastructure that demonstrates to funders that their investment is safe.

Here's the checklist I walk every client through before we write a single word of a proposal.

## The Eight Pillars of Grant Readiness

### 1. Legal standing
You have your IRS determination letter. Your state registration is current. You're in good standing with both. If you're operating under a fiscal sponsor, the agreement is documented and current.

### 2. Current bylaws
Your bylaws reflect how the organization actually operates. They've been reviewed — ideally in the last two years. They define a clear decision-making process and include a conflict-of-interest policy.

### 3. Active, engaged board
You have at least three board members who are not related to each other or to staff. They attend meetings. They understand their fiduciary duties. They've signed a conflict-of-interest disclosure.

### 4. Current 990 or 990-N
Your most recent 990 is filed and accurate. If you're under the $50k threshold, your 990-N (e-Postcard) is filed on time. Funders look this up before reading your proposal.

### 5. Audited or reviewed financials
For grants over $100k, many funders require an independent audit. For smaller grants, a financial review or compilation may suffice. Either way, your books are clean, organized, and current.

### 6. A documented strategic plan
Even a one-page plan is better than none. Funders want to know where you're going and how you're planning to get there. This also makes proposal writing dramatically easier.

### 7. A clear program model
You can explain what you do, who you serve, what outputs you produce, and what outcomes you're working toward — in three sentences or less. If you can't, tighten it before you apply.

### 8. A case for support
A written document — two to four pages — that tells your story, articulates the need, and makes the argument for investment. Think of it as the foundation every proposal is built on.

## What to Do If You're Not Ready Yet

Don't wait until everything is perfect to start building. Each of these pillars can be addressed one at a time. Start with your legal standing, then your governance documents, then your financials.

In the meantime, look at smaller community foundations and local funders who work with emerging organizations. Many of them have capacity-building grants specifically designed to help organizations become grant-ready.

The goal isn't to be ready for every funder at once. It's to be ready for the right one at the right time.
